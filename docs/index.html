Population Clustering Visualizations

These visualizations show Earth's population distribution through the lens of a gravitational potential field. Each populated area creates a "pull" that decreases with the cube of distance (1/d³), similar to how gravity works but with population density instead of mass.

Think of it like a watershed: Population centers are valleys, remote areas are peaks, and natural boundaries form at the drainage divides. To find where NYC's influence ends and Mexico City's begins, imagine filling each valley with water until the lakes just touch - that's the watershed boundary.

What you're seeing: Not population density, but population structure - the natural hierarchy of how settlements connect and cluster. The Himalayas appear as ridges separating India from China. The Grand Trunk Road emerges as a connecting valley wall between Delhi, Kolkata, and cities in between - the corridor itself becomes visible as a structural feature, not just implied by city peaks. Rivers, coastlines, and transportation corridors show up naturally as the paths where influence flows.

Why this matters: Unlike density maps that dissolve into dots at high resolution, this structure persists across scales. Zoom out: see continental watersheds. Zoom in: see regional corridors. Zoom further: see neighborhood connections. The hierarchy remains meaningful at every level - it doesn't turn into noise.

Data source: NASA GPWv4 population grid (2020), 15 arc-minute resolution

World Population Structure
